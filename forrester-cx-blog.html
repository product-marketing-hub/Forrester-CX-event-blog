How to stop AI
breaking the brand

You can no longer control the AI customer journey. Notes from Papirfly's spotlight session on why guiding it — not controlling it — is now the CX leader's job.

FT
Frank Tommy VP Customer Engagement & Activation, Papirfly · 9 min read

This June, several thousand customer experience, marketing and digital leaders gathered in Amsterdam for Forrester's CX Summit EMEA. The theme was deliberately provocative: Build the experience AI can't.

It's a sharp framing for the moment we're in. As Forrester put it, CX, marketing and digital teams are racing to build smarter journeys, automate service, deploy agents and personalise at scale — all while consumer trust sits at an all-time low. The summit's argument was that beneath every intelligent experience lies something AI cannot invent, infer or magically repair: a foundation of human creativity, trust, context, customer identity and quality data. One of the keynote lines stuck with the whole room — distrust is now consumers' default. As AI-generated content, deepfakes and automation blur what's real, trust is no longer assumed. It has to be earned.

That backdrop is exactly why Papirfly was there as a Forrester partner — and why our spotlight session focused on one specific, uncomfortable consequence of this shift for anyone who owns a brand.

The Forrester model

Total Experience is built from three layers

Forrester frames the discipline around Total Experience — the idea that what a customer ultimately feels about you is the sum of three overlapping layers, not any single department's output. This year Forrester even expanded its Total Experience Score with a new Employee Experience Index, making the link between the layers measurable.

Forrester's Total Experience: employee, customer and brand experience converging into one perception. Adapted from the CX Summit EMEA stage.

Employee experience and customer experience get most of the airtime. But the third layer — brand experience — is where this year's AI story really bites. Brand experience is no longer just what your audience sees in a campaign. It's what every system, every channel and increasingly every machine understands about you.

The Papirfly spotlight

You can't control the AI customer journey. Your job is to guide it.

Our spotlight session sat squarely on that brand experience layer, under a deliberately blunt title: How to stop AI breaking the brand. The premise is simple to state and hard to act on. The customer journey you've spent years mapping — discover, evaluate, buy, onboard, use, renew — is increasingly mediated by an AI assistant you don't own and can't see into. You cannot control that journey anymore. What you can do is guide it, by being deliberate about every signal the machine reads.

What follows is the argument we walked the room through, slide by slide.

Your customer decided today — without ever speaking to you

Start with the thing that's already happening. Somewhere today, one of your customers bought, renewed or churned. They never spoke to your team. They asked an AI assistant, read its one-paragraph answer, and acted on it. AI shaped what they believed about you — and you weren't in the room. That's not a future scenario to plan for. It's the current default for a growing share of decisions.

Your customer bought, renewed, or churned today. But they never spoke to your team. AI shaped what they believed.

The new interface is the AI assistant

A decade ago, almost every journey started in a browser and a search box. Today it increasingly starts inside ChatGPT, Claude or Gemini. The assistant has become the new interface — the layer customers and colleagues meet your brand through before they ever reach a page you built. And here's the shift that matters for CX: the assistant doesn't just retrieve your homepage. It reads everything — your comparison pages, pricing, onboarding flows, support articles, product notes — and synthesises one answer.

That changes who owns the brand. The content feeding these systems isn't produced only by brand and marketing teams. It's produced across the entire organisation — by support, by product, by sales, by partners. For an AI to understand anything coherent about your brand, no matter who published the underlying signal, there has to be a layer beneath the journey that is genuinely consistent: a consistency layer that can be read and understood by machines.

The journey you own sits between two forces: the AI interface on top, and the machine-readable consistency layer underneath.
Why it matters

Consistency creates clarity. Clarity is what AI trusts.

Why does this consistency layer matter so much? Because of how these systems actually decide what to say about you. An AI assistant doesn't look you up in a single tidy record. It reconstructs an entity — a picture of who you are — from fragments scattered across everything it has read. Consistency is what lets it resolve those fragments into one confident, coherent answer. Inconsistency does the opposite: it forces the machine to guess, and it guesses confidently.

This isn't only a branding instinct. A growing body of research on how large language models select and cite sources points to the same mechanism. Recent academic work describes a model's confidence in surfacing a brand as resting on whether its signals are consistently structured across its entire digital footprint. When they are, the model's threshold for citing — and recommending — that brand rises. The practical version of that idea breaks into four layers worth understanding:

01

Identity

The non-negotiable facts: who you are, what you're called, what you do. Stated the same way everywhere, so you're recognisable as one distinct entity rather than several blurry ones.

02

Relationships

How you connect to people, products, parent and sister brands. The machine-readable graph that stops an assistant from confusing you with a similarly named competitor.

03

Offering

A clear, consistent description of what you actually sell, in language that maps cleanly to how customers ask. Vague positioning doesn't get extracted; specific, repeated claims do.

04

Reputation

The third-party corroboration — reviews, coverage, mentions — that confirms the story you tell about yourself. Consistent signals across independent sources read as trustworthy.

The numbers behind this are still early and fast-moving, but the direction is consistent. In one large analysis of how assistants cite sources, a brand's strength as a recognised entity was the single strongest predictor of being cited — ahead of every traditional technical signal. Brands showing up consistently across four or more independent sources were meaningfully more likely to appear in an assistant's answer than those present only on their own site. The lesson isn't "publish more." It's "be the same, everywhere."

The reframe

Branding isn't what Marketing creates. It's what your customer — or an AI — experiences at every step.

Every touchpoint is now training data

Here's the part that reframes the whole problem. It's tempting to think the AI only reads the "official" brand assets — the website, the campaign, the polished deck. It doesn't. It reads everything. The obvious things: your website, product UI, display ads, social posts, press coverage. And the non-obvious things most teams never think of as brand: onboarding emails, support docs, chatbot replies, invoices, reward-programme terms, packaging copy, newsletters, the forum thread where someone described what you do, a single sentence a rep once put in writing to a customer.

All of it is feeding the machine. And all of it gets compressed into one synthesised sentence handed to your customer. If those thousands of signals point in slightly different directions, the machine resolves the contradiction for you — and you may not like the answer it lands on. That's why the consistency layer isn't a nice-to-have. It's the only thing that keeps a thousand scattered touchpoints from averaging out into a muddled, or simply wrong, recommendation.

The old world forgave you. The new world doesn't.

The reason this feels new is that the stakes of inconsistency have fundamentally changed.

Old world — until 2024

Customers found you, compared you, decided.

Inconsistency was annoying, but humans did the synthesis themselves.

They filled the gaps, gave you the benefit of the doubt, and forgave the rough edges.

New world — 2026 and beyond

An AI reads everything you've ever published, then decides for them.

The customer sees a one-sentence synthesis of every signal you've put out.

Inconsistency isn't dilutive anymore. It's disqualifying.

That's the whole shift in one line. When a human did the synthesising, gaps were forgivable. When a machine does it, gaps become contradictions, and contradictions get resolved against you — often by leaving you out of the answer entirely.

What this means for you

The mandate hasn't changed. The terrain has.

You still own the customer relationship. That hasn't moved. But the ground you operate on has shifted — and it lands a little differently depending on the seat you're in at an event like CX Summit.

So how do you scale consistency?
Enable anyone to create content.

This is the part that challenges most people's instincts. The reflex response to "we have a consistency problem" is to centralise — to funnel everything through a small brand team that checks every asset. That doesn't scale, and it never will, because the volume of touchpoints is exploding faster than any central team can review.

The only way to scale consistency is to do the opposite: enable absolutely anyone to create content — every team, every region, every partner, and increasingly AI agents acting on your behalf — while making it almost impossible for them to go off-brand. The way you reconcile "anyone can create" with "everything stays consistent" is to build every output from a single source of truth: your brand, expressed as structured, governed brand data. When the brand is the foundation everything is built from, consistency stops being a manual check at the end and becomes a property of the system itself.

The capabilities that make it work

Four things have to be true

Enabling anyone to create, without losing the brand, only works if four capabilities are genuinely in place. This is the difference between a template tool and a consistency engine.

Enforce brand rules

Lock what must never change — logo, clear space, colours, typography — while leaving defined room to flex within the brand framework. Off-brand simply becomes impossible, not just discouraged.

Localise at scale

Adapt language, format and size to local needs — German, English, French, every channel ratio — from one master, so local teams get autonomy and the centre keeps control.

Support every channel

Social, email, print, web, video — one source produces every output a modern journey needs, so the brand stays coherent no matter where the customer or the machine encounters it.

Integrate with your martech

Tight upstream and downstream integration with the tools your teams already use — so consistency lives inside the day-to-day workflow rather than bolted on as an extra step.

Then — and only then — you can scale

Put those pieces together and something important becomes possible. You can scale your content creation across the whole organisation and its partners without scaling chaos. Every asset, in every language, on every channel, made by anyone, still reads as one coherent brand. That coherence is precisely the consistency layer the machines now require. You're not choosing between speed and control anymore — the system gives you both, which is the only way to feed a thousand consistent signals into the AI that's deciding your customer's next move.
